Introduction: A Storyline & Modeling Approach to Chemistry in Pennsylvania

Philosophy of this Course

This chemistry course is designed to immerse students in the role of sense-makers and problem-solvers. Each unit is launched by an engaging, complex phenomenon—often rooted in Pennsylvania's unique industrial, commercial, and manufacturing contexts—that ignites student curiosity. These authentic questions then drive a coherent storyline of investigation, evidence-gathering, and collaborative modeling. Students actively construct their understanding of core chemical principles, not as isolated facts, but as tools to explain the initial phenomenon and address broader, related questions.

The pedagogical framework draws heavily from the American Modeling Teachers Association (AMTA) curriculum, which emphasizes that students develop deep conceptual understanding by building, testing, and refining explanatory models. This student-centered approach is fully aligned with the Pennsylvania Science, Technology & Engineering, Environmental Literacy & Sustainability (STEELS) standards, as detailed in the PA Department of Education's High School Physical Science Curriculum Framework. These standards champion three-dimensional learning: the integration of Disciplinary Core Ideas (DCIs), Science and Engineering Practices (SEPs), and Crosscutting Concepts (CCCs). This NGSS-aligned, storyline-driven structure aims to make chemistry relevant, engaging, and intellectually stimulating, preparing students to apply scientific reasoning to real-world challenges.

Pennsylvania Context and Course Goals

Recognizing Pennsylvania's rich heritage in industry and manufacturing, its diverse natural resources, and its contemporary environmental and energy landscapes, this course seeks to connect chemistry to students' lived experiences. By grounding phenomena and applications in the Commonwealth's industrial history—from the iron furnaces of Wrightsville and Columbia to the modern manufacturing and food processing plants of York County—we aim to demonstrate the immediate relevance of chemistry. The course is paced for a single semester with daily 70-minute classes, allowing for in-depth exploration and modeling activities.

Key goals include enabling students to:

  • Explain chemical phenomena using progressively sophisticated particle and energy models.
  • Master PA STEELs Science and Engineering Practices through authentic inquiry, as outlined in the PA Physical Science Framework.
  • Apply Crosscutting Concepts detailed in the PA Framework to analyze and connect chemical systems.
  • Understand the societal and industrial implications of chemistry, particularly within PA, integrating ELS and T&E standards.

Course Overview: A Semester of Chemical Storylines (PA STEELS & SBG Integrated)

This semester-long chemistry course is now structured into five major thematic units to ensure more rigorous and comprehensive coverage of the PA STEELS standards. Each unit is designed to progressively build students' understanding of fundamental chemical principles by investigating compelling questions related to Pennsylvania's industrial, commercial, and technological landscape. The content, including Big Ideas, Essential Questions, specific standard language (DCIs, SEPs, CCCs), and Vocabulary, is directly drawn from or strongly aligned with the Pennsylvania Department of Education's High School Physical Science Curriculum Framework for STEELS standards.

A core component of this curriculum is its commitment to **Standards-Based Grading (SBG)**. Student progress will be measured against specific PA STEELS Performance Expectations. The focus is on demonstrating proficiency and growth over time, with opportunities for reassessment. This approach separates academic achievement from work habits, providing a clearer picture of what students know and can do. Performance will be evaluated using a diverse suite of tools, including DCI-focused performance tasks, Wonder of Science 3D assessments, AMTA content tests, and Quizizz learning checks. Student learning will also be measured and guided by resources from the PA SAS (Standards Aligned System) portal, including state-provided rubrics and assessment item samplers.

The Model So Far: A Progression

This section outlines the progressive development of our scientific model of matter, a core component of the AMTA (American Modeling Teachers Association) curriculum. With each unit, we add new features to our model based on new evidence, allowing us to explain more complex phenomena.

After Unit: Matter

The Simple Particle Model & Kinetic Molecular Theory (KMT)

  • All matter is composed of tiny, individual particles (atoms or molecules).
  • These particles are in constant, random motion. Their average kinetic energy is proportional to temperature.
  • There is empty space between the particles. The amount of space differs for solids, liquids, and gases.
  • Particles collide with each other and the walls of their container, creating pressure (for gases).
  • The relationships between Pressure (P), Volume (V), and Temperature (T) for a gas can be predicted based on the motion and collisions of these particles.

After Unit: Atoms

The Nuclear Atom Model

  • The particles (atoms) have internal structure.
  • At the center of each atom is a tiny, dense, positively-charged nucleus, which contains protons (p⁺) and neutrons (n⁰).
  • The number of protons defines the element's identity (atomic number).
  • Surrounding the nucleus are negatively-charged electrons (e⁻) in specific energy levels or shells.
  • In a neutral atom, the number of electrons equals the number of protons.
  • Isotopes of an element have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. Some isotopes have unstable nuclei and undergo radioactive decay.

After Unit: Bonding

The Bonding & Intermolecular Forces (IMF) Model

  • The electrons in the outermost energy level (valence electrons) are responsible for chemical bonding.
  • Atoms bond to achieve a more stable electron configuration, typically by gaining, losing (ionic bonding), or sharing (covalent bonding) valence electrons.
  • The 3D shape of a molecule is determined by the repulsion between electron pairs (VSEPR theory).
  • The sharing of electrons can be unequal (polar covalent) or equal (nonpolar covalent), creating bond dipoles.
  • The overall shape and bond polarities determine if a molecule is polar or nonpolar.
  • Weak attractive forces, called intermolecular forces (IMFs), exist *between* molecules and determine bulk properties like boiling point and solubility.

After Unit: Reactions

The Conservation Model

  • Chemical reactions are processes that rearrange atoms to form new substances.
  • Atoms are not created or destroyed in a chemical reaction; they are conserved.
  • A balanced chemical equation is a model that represents this conservation of atoms.
  • The mole is a quantitative unit that connects the atomic scale (number of particles) to the macroscopic scale (mass in grams).
  • The ratios in a balanced equation represent mole ratios, allowing for quantitative predictions (stoichiometry).

After Unit: Chemical Engineering Concepts

The Dynamic Reaction Model

  • Energy is stored in chemical bonds. The overall energy change of a reaction (enthalpy, ΔH) depends on the energy required to break bonds versus the energy released when forming new ones.
  • For a reaction to occur, particles must collide with sufficient energy (activation energy) and correct orientation (Collision Theory).
  • The rate of a reaction can be controlled by changing factors that affect collision frequency and energy (temperature, concentration, surface area, catalysts).
  • Many reactions are reversible and can reach a state of dynamic equilibrium where the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal.
  • A system at equilibrium will shift to counteract a stress (Le Châtelier's Principle), allowing engineers to control the yield of a reaction.

Conclusion: Cultivating Chemical Thinkers for Pennsylvania's Future

This NGSS storyline-driven, AMTA-inspired chemistry course, now deeply integrated with the Pennsylvania Department of Education's High School Physical Science Curriculum Framework for STEELS standards, is meticulously designed to foster a robust and applicable understanding of chemical principles. By centering learning around compelling, locally relevant phenomena and guiding students through cycles of inquiry, modeling, and argumentation, the course aims to move beyond rote memorization towards genuine scientific sense-making. The explicit inclusion of PA's official Big Ideas, Essential Questions, standard-specific DCIs, SEPs, CCCs, and vocabulary ensures that students are not only mastering core chemical concepts but are also becoming proficient in the cognitive tools and practices outlined by the Commonwealth for science education.

The explicit integration of Environmental Literacy & Sustainability (ELS) and Technology & Engineering (T&E) standards throughout the units—from investigating manufacturing quality control and material properties to exploring industrial processes and sustainable design—highlights the profound impact of chemistry on society and the economy. Students are challenged to consider the broader implications of chemical knowledge, preparing them to be responsible and informed citizens equipped to engage with Pennsylvania's unique industrial and technological landscape.

The adoption of a **Standards-Based Grading** framework, supported by authentic performance assessments, ensures that evaluation is focused on student proficiency and growth. The overall assessment strategy is guided by the performance measures, item samplers, and rubrics available through the Pennsylvania SAS portal to ensure alignment with state expectations. Regular review and practice with questions formatted similarly to those on the PA Keystone Exams will be integrated to support student success on state-level assessments.

Ultimately, this course endeavors to equip Pennsylvania students with not just a knowledge of chemistry, but with the critical thinking, problem-solving, and collaborative skills inherent in scientific inquiry. They will learn to see chemistry not as an abstract subject, but as a vital tool for understanding their world, addressing its challenges, and contributing to a sustainable future for the Commonwealth and beyond, fully aligned with the vision of the PA STEELS standards.
